# Advanced Tire Mechanics 1: Snow

## Summary

This source covers tire traction on snowy roads, with emphasis on the difficulty of improving multiple road-condition performances simultaneously. It introduces a design trade-off in which increasing [[Void Ratio]] tends to improve [[Snow Traction]] but reduce [[Ice Traction]] because contact area on ice decreases.

The source presents friction mechanisms of rubber on snow, then develops an [[Analytical Model of Tire Traction on Snow]] in which snow traction is decomposed into [[Shear Force of Snow in Void]], [[Frictional Force Between Tread and Snow]], [[Plowing Force]] or [[Edge Effect]], and [[Braking Force from Snow Compression]]. It also explains how [[Slip Ratio]] changes the active force components: the void-shear contribution is important at small slip ratio but disappears at large slip ratio after snow in the void fractures.

The source also discusses [[FEA of Tire Traction on Snow]] using a coupled Lagrangian tire and Eulerian/FVM snow representation. The simulation focuses on snow shear in voids, uses a Mohr-Coulomb snow model, and is validated qualitatively against shear-box behavior, snow surface deformation, and experimental tendencies for [[Lateral Groove Width]] and [[Lug Angle]].

## Key Claims

- Increasing [[Void Ratio]] improves [[Snow Traction]] but decreases [[Ice Traction]] by reducing contact area with ice.
- [[Analytical Model of Tire Traction on Snow]] represents longitudinal snow traction as a combination of four forces.
- [[Shear Force of Snow in Void]] contributes at small [[Slip Ratio]] but not at large slip ratio, because snow in the void fractures under high slip.
- [[Plowing Force]] is generated by block and sipe edges and is approximately proportional to total lateral edge length in the contact footprint under the cited packed-snow conditions.
- [[Void Shrinkage]] creates an optimum for void shear force because snow compaction increases [[Snow Shear Strength]] while reducing available shear area.
- [[FEA of Tire Traction on Snow]] can visualize otherwise unobservable contact-patch shear stress and help compare practical tread patterns.

## Design Implications

- Snow tire design should treat [[Void Ratio]] as a trade-off parameter rather than a one-direction improvement lever.
- Optimizing snow traction requires balancing void volume, void shrinkage, edge length, and contact area.
- [[Tire Tread Pattern]] can be designed to increase lateral edge contribution and useful snow shear without relying only on higher void ratio.
- Simulation is useful for pattern screening, especially where experiments are seasonal or difficult to instrument.
